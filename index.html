Top War · Decor Set Store · A growth proposal

The sets are built.
The store is empty.

Twenty-three decoration sets exist in Top War. Only eight are reachable through the Decor Set Store, where players spend the tokens they earn and buy. The rest sit on a shelf — and the most engaged players have run out of things to buy. This is a look at what that costs, and why a steady release cadence earns more than holding sets back.

01
The store today

A catalogue mostly behind glass

The Decor Set Store sells sets for Decor Set Tokens — a soft currency players earn weekly and can top up with small purchases. It's the affordable, repeatable path to decorations. Of the 23 sets that exist, only these are currently buyable there:

In the affordable token store 8 SETS · ON ROTATION
Older sets shelved from the store 10 SETS · 2021–2024 · THE BACKLOG
Recent sets — premium for now 5 SETS · USD PACKS · NOT CONTESTED

The point isn't the new sets. Releasing the freshest sets as premium USD bundles first is reasonable — that's where launch revenue lives, and this proposal leaves it alone. The issue is the ten older sets, some dating back to 2021–2023, that also never reached the token store. Their premium window closed long ago, yet anyone who missed them still has no affordable path to them.

The store only ever cycles the same eight on rotation. By players' accounts, roughly no new set has entered the token store in the last year — so the older backlog just keeps growing while the eight reshuffle. The fix is simple: each time a new set launches, let the oldest shelved one graduate into the store — at the pace the studio already ships.

A new set ships every 2–3 months (≈4–5 a year) — yet the token store hasn't gained one in roughly a year. 05
The behaviour

Two players, one trigger

Steady releases work because both kinds of paying players respond to the same thing: a reward that's almost in reach.

The patient spender

Closes the gap a dollar at a time

Earns free tokens, tops up with the $1 pack five times a week to inch toward the next set. Low monthly spend, but reliable — month after month, as long as the next set is there to chase.

Fires when: a set is weeks away, not years.
The impatient spender

Pays to skip the wait

Sees a set they don't own and buys the bigger pack to have it now. Higher spend per event — but only triggered by a set that's freshly available and still missing from their collection.

Fires when: there's something new they don't have yet.

Withhold the sets and neither player has a reason to spend. The shelf is full; the goal is gone.

06
The case

No one gains from holding sets back

A steady release cadence isn't a giveaway — it's the version of this system where both sides come out ahead.

For the studio
  • Steadier revenue. A standing goal in the store keeps the weekly drip and the impulse buys flowing instead of stalling.
  • More lifetime value, less waste. Tokens get spent toward goals rather than banked and redeemed for free at a dump.
  • Re-engages your best players. The ones who've collected everything are exactly the ones with nothing to buy right now.
For players
  • A narrowing gap. Every decoration set carries a permanent march-size bonus; access to more sets means the spread between collections closes over time.
  • A reason to keep playing. A fresh goal on the horizon instead of a shelf they can only look at.
  • A fair path. Earned tokens and small spend both lead somewhere, at any budget.
The proposal
Each time a new set launches, let the oldest shelved set graduate into the token store — one-in, one-graduates — while the newest stay premium and the monthly pop-up packs carry on. Do it before the backlog and the stockpiles grow past the point of being worth chasing.

This asks nothing of the new-set revenue and nothing of the pop-up packs. The older sets are already made, their launch window is years gone, and putting them in front of players at the pace the studio already ships costs nothing to build, earns more than holding them, and quietly closes a growing gap. There's no version of the math where the freeze comes out ahead.
