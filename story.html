A level worth
chasing.

A commander finished a set, then ran into a wall trying to max it. Nobody pays hundreds in one lump for plus two march size. But a few dollars a week toward a visible goal? That, they pay gladly, week after week.

Told by a Server 2864 player
1 Meet the commander

A collector who finally hit a ceiling.

Commander Birch

Server 2864 · playing since 2022 · mid tier spender
"I completed Sweet Haven and got my plus two march size. Now I want to max it. The only path is a wall of cash, so I just stopped."

Birch is a made up handle standing in for thousands of steady, friendly players who keep a server alive. They are not a whale and not a free rider. They log in, they earn, and they like having a goal in front of them.

Decoration sets are perfect for that, because they reward you for collecting more than once. Complete every piece of a set and it reaches Level 1, a permanent plus two march size that applies the moment the set is complete, with no placement needed. Collect duplicate full sets and the set levels up: Level 2 is plus four, and the rare top sets reach Level 3 for plus six. That is the chase Birch loves. The trouble is reaching the higher levels.

Each level is more march size, permanent, and march size is the stat collectors chase hardest. Of the 23 sets in the game, only two can ever reach Level 3: Metal Music Fans (released May 2024) and Sweet Haven (released December 2025). The other 21 sets cap at Level 2. Birch is sitting on a complete Sweet Haven, staring at plus six, and there is no friendly way to climb.

2 The leveling ladder

Each level is more duplicate sets, paid in tokens.

Leveling is collecting the same full set again and again. In the store currency, Decor Set Tokens, the cost of reaching each level follows a clean rule: 11,340 tokens times (2 times level minus 1). One full set is 11,340 tokens, about $94 at the cheapest pack rate.

The shape of the problem is the same either way: every level up is a meaningful pile of currency, and a collector like Birch wants to keep climbing.

3 The wall nobody pays

The lump sum almost no one will pay for plus two.

There are two ways to fund a level up. One asks for hundreds of dollars in a single moment. One asks for about five dollars a week toward a goal you can watch fill. Players react very differently to each.

The lump-sum wall
~$175 to ~$875
paid all at once through launch packs, at about $175 per full set
  • Level 1 about $175, Level 2 about $525, Level 3 about $875
  • A single plus two march level up asks for hundreds in one go
  • Time limited: when a set's launch window closes, this door closes too
The weekly installment
~$5 / week
small top ups toward a visible goal in the store, plus free tokens
  • The $0.99 pack: 120 tokens, capped at 5 a week, about $4.95 for 600 tokens
  • Plus about 300 free tokens a week, so about 900 tokens land each week
  • The catch: there is currently nothing in the store to grind a level toward

This is the sharpest line in the whole story. A plus two march size level up is something almost nobody will pay a $350 to $875 lump sum for, but a roughly five dollar a week installment toward a goal they can see filling is something a spender pays gladly, week after week. That weekly drip is exactly the sticky, recurring revenue and the retention that the current design leaves on the table.

For Birch, the wall is plain math. Pushing Sweet Haven from a freshly completed Level 1 toward plus six means thousands more tokens, and the only path on offer is a stack of expensive launch packs that, for older sets, is not even available anymore. So a willing, happy customer simply walks away from the climb.

4 Birch's installment journey

Five dollars a week, a level in reach.

Picture the friendly version. Sweet Haven is reachable in the store, Birch sets a goal, and each week the bar creeps forward: about 300 free tokens plus a $4.95 top up, about 900 tokens a week. Here is what the climb actually looks like.

A $129 climb spread over six months is invisible to a player's wallet and golden to the studio. The same plus two march that nobody buys as a lump sum becomes an easy, recurring habit. And a collector chasing plus six on Sweet Haven simply renews that habit for the next level, and the next.

6 The recurring revenue view

A habit is worth more than a sale.

Zoom out from Birch to a server full of collectors. The lump-sum model captures a rare big spender once and then goes quiet. The installment model captures a small, steady payment from many players, every single week, for as long as there is a level to chase.

The lifetime value framing is the whole point. A one time launch pack ends. A leveling habit renews. A spender who always has a level in front of them keeps paying about $4.95 a week for as long as the chase exists, which is exactly the recurring revenue and retention a healthy live game is built on.

7 The prerequisite

You can only grind a set you can reach.

Leveling only works if the set is reachable in the store in the first place. That is where the older sets come in. Right now the token store cycles the same eight sets on rotation, while the newest sets stay premium and the oldest sets sit shelved with no affordable path. A set has to graduate into the store before anyone can grind it to max level.

So the access idea is simple and supporting, not the headline. Each time a new set launches, about every 2.5 months, the oldest shelved set graduates into the token store. That keeps the freshest content premium, keeps the launch packs intact, and gives patient players a set they are finally allowed to chase, and then to level. Access is the door. Leveling is the room behind it.

9 The single ask
Let players climb in installments

Let a set reach its max level through the store, paid weekly.

Open a path to a set's maximum level through the Decor Set Store, funded in small weekly token installments, instead of only through expensive time limited launch packs or, once a window closes, not at all. The newest sets stay premium, the launch packs stay intact, and players gain a level they can chase about five dollars at a time. As a supporting step, let the oldest shelved set graduate into the store with each launch, so there is always a fresh set to climb.

10 The fair question

Won't a token path undercut pack sales?

It is the right question to ask, and the honest answer is no. A weekly installment toward leveling reaches a different player, in a different moment, than the premium packs do.

Only older windows open up
The newest five sets stay premium and time limited. Anyone who wants a fresh set right now keeps buying launch packs exactly as they do today. Nothing fresh leaves the premium shelf.
The installment path takes months
A single level up is about six months of patient five dollar weeks, about $129. Players who want a level now still pay the lump sum to skip the wait. The installment never beats the pack on speed.
Installments grow revenue, not shift it
About $69,000 a year in $0.99-pack installments is money the studio earns about $0 of today, because there is no level to grind toward. It is additive to launch and bundle revenue, never a substitute for it.
The wall sale rarely happens anyway
Almost nobody pays $350 to $875 in a lump sum for plus two march size. Capturing about five dollars a week from a willing player is found revenue, not a discount on a sale that was going to happen.

A weekly installment reaches the patient earner who was never going to pay a full launch lump sum anyway. It does not move a single premium buyer onto the slow path. It turns a dead end into a small, steady, recurring source of spend, and it keeps a softly widening power gap from leaving mid tier players behind.

Give Birch a level worth chasing.

The content already exists. The currency already flows. The willing players are already here, logging in and earning toward a goal that is currently behind an unaffordable wall. Let them climb in small weekly installments and a quiet shelf becomes a steady, friendly, recurring source of revenue, while a drifting collector turns back into a happy one.

Thank you for reading, and for building a game worth collecting in.
