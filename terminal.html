01

Set leveling: the permanent stat almost nobody can pay for

SOURCE: IN-GAME ECONOMY

Completing all pieces of a decoration set is Level 1: a permanent +2 March Size, granted on completion, no placement needed. Each further level adds +2 more march size (Level 1 is +2, Level 2 is +4, Level 3 is +6). You level up by collecting duplicate FULL sets. So a level-up is a real, permanent gameplay reward that the most engaged players want, and the only way to buy it fast today is a launch-pack lump sum that the data says they will not pay.

Reachable levels, confirmed: only two of the 23 sets can reach Level 3, "Metal Music Fans" (released May 2024) and "Sweet Haven" (released Dec 2025). The other 21 sets cap at Level 2, which is +4 march size. No other set reaches Level 3.

Tokens to reach a level follow 11,340 times (2 times level minus 1): Level 1 is 11,340 tokens, which is one full set and about $94 at the cheapest pack rate. Level 2 is 34,020 tokens, three full sets. Level 3 is 56,700 tokens, five full sets. Going Level 1 to Level 2 is two more full sets, plus 22,680 tokens, which is confirmed. The Level 2 to Level 3 step shown here assumes two more sets again and is illustrative and adjustable wherever a Level-3-specific cost appears.

The defect: the only fast path to any of these levels is a launch-pack lump sum at about $175 per full set, roughly $175 for Level 1, $525 for Level 2, $875 for Level 3. That is a wall almost nobody pays for a +2 march-size tick. There is no installment lane in the store to grind a set toward its max level, so the recurring spend that this reward should generate currently reads as zero.

02

The pivot: lump-sum wall versus weekly installment

HEADLINE METRIC: INSTALLMENT LTV

A +2 march-size level-up is something almost nobody will pay a $350 to $875 lump sum for. A roughly $5-a-week installment toward a visible goal is something a spender pays gladly, week after week. That is exactly the sticky recurring revenue and retention the current design throws away. The installment lane is the $0.99 pack: 120 tokens, capped at 5 a week, about 600 paid tokens for about $4.95 a week, plus about 300 free tokens a week, so about 900 tokens a week toward a chosen level.

03

Access first: completion revenue, hold-and-dump versus steady graduation

RECOMPUTES ON INPUT

Leveling only works if a set is reachable in the store in the first place. That is the access prerequisite: one set graduates into the store each time a new set launches, about every 2.5 months, so players can complete it and then grind it toward max level. This second model prices the completion revenue of that access on its own. A per-player simulation across 52 weeks, summed over a population. Each week a player banks 300 free tokens. If an unowned, available older set exists and the player is a payer, they add their tier's paid tokens and spend that tier's cash, then complete as many full sets as their balance allows. With no available goal, paid buying stops and free tokens pile up as stranded balance. Adjust the inputs; the chart and tables update live. Every dollar here is illustrative completion revenue from the older backlog only, separate from and additive to the leveling installments above.

05

Cannibalization stress-test

PESSIMISTIC CASE

The fair objection: do free-earned token sets and installment leveling undercut premium pack sales? We load the worst-case assumptions and the position still clears.

Why pack revenue holds

  • Only the oldest graduates. Recent and current sets stay premium. The newest 5 are never in scope, so their launch windows are untouched.
  • Closed windows sell nothing today. An older set whose launch window shut years ago books no pack revenue now, so installment leveling on it is pure upside, not a displaced sale.
  • The installment path still takes months. Impatient and competitive players keep buying packs to skip the wait to a level.
  • Pop-up packs bundle far more than the bare set: extra currency, speedups, and exclusives. Those keep selling.

Who installment leveling actually reaches

  • The patient spender who was never going to pay a $525 to $875 lump for +4 or +6 march. Today they pay nothing toward it.
  • Lapsing mid-tier players given a reachable, visible goal, which lifts session frequency and weekly soft-currency top-ups.
  • A converted weekly installment buyer is incremental recurring revenue, since the alternative is zero, not a lost pack sale.
  • The whale segment grows, not shrinks: a level to chase at about $5 a week keeps them paying every week, on top of any day-one pack buying.
STRESS RESULT: even crediting the displaced-pack-sale objection in full, every graduated completion sale and every weekly installment is incremental against a baseline of $0 on shelved sets and on leveling. The model's downside is bounded by zero; the upside is the illustrative +$47,676 completion delta plus an illustrative ~$69,000 a year installment pool. Position survives the pessimistic case.
