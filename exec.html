Statement of recurring revenue, currently booked at zero

A small weekly installment toward a visible goal is something a spender pays gladly, week after week.

A completed decoration set grants a permanent army size boost, and that set can be leveled by collecting duplicate full sets, each level adding more march size for good. Almost nobody will pay a single lump sum of hundreds of dollars for one such level. But a roughly $5 a week installment toward a level they can see filling up is sticky, recurring revenue that a spender pays happily. Right now there is nothing to grind toward through the store, so this entire recurring line is booked at $0. The numbers below are revenue already in reach, waiting to be switched on.

I

The level chase: a recurring line we never opened

Completing every piece of a decoration set is Level 1, a permanent plus 2 march size with no placement needed. Each further level adds plus 2 more, and a level is reached by collecting duplicate full sets. The only paths to a level today are expensive one-off launch packs, or, for older sets, nothing at all once the window closes.

Here is the mechanic in plain terms. One full set is 11,340 tokens and grants Level 1, +2 march size forever. Three full sets reach Level 2 at 34,020 tokens for +4 march. Five full sets reach Level 3 at 56,700 tokens for +6 march. Only two of the 23 sets can reach Level 3 at all: Metal Music Fans (released May 2024) and Sweet Haven (released Dec 2025). The other 21 sets cap at Level 2.

L3 reachable

Only Metal Music Fans and Sweet Haven can reach Level 3 (+6 march, 56,700 tokens). Every other set tops out at Level 2 (+4 march, 34,020 tokens). The Level 1 to Level 2 step is two more full sets, plus 22,680 tokens, confirmed. The Level 2 to Level 3 step shown here as two more sets again is illustrative and adjustable.

The contrast is the whole argument. Set leveling for march size is a benefit a spender genuinely wants, but the lump sum to buy it outright is a wall almost nobody pays. The same spender will pay a small weekly installment toward the same goal, gladly, for months.

Now read it as lifetime value. A committed spender who keeps a level chase going pays about $4.95 a week indefinitely, about $257 a year, every year. On an illustrative population of 600 active players with about 45% paying, that is roughly $69,000 a year in pure $0.99-pack installments. Today that line reads about $0, because there is nothing in the store to grind toward. This recurring stream is fully additive to the set-completion revenue covered in the next sections, and the two should never be conflated.

VI

The ask

No new build, no discount to premium, no change to live operations. Let players reach a set's max level through the store, paid in small weekly installments, with older sets graduating in to make that level reachable.
Recommendation

Let players reach a set's max level through the store, in small weekly token installments, instead of only through expensive launch packs or not at all.

A plus 2 march level-up is something almost nobody will pay a $350 to $875 lump sum for. The same goal at about $5 a week toward a visible target is something a spender pays gladly, week after week. That is the sticky, recurring revenue and retention the current design throws away. Selling levels as installments is the headline.

The supporting prerequisite is access: a level can only be chased once its set is reachable in the store. So tie graduation to the cadence that already exists. Each time a brand-new set launches, the oldest shelved set graduates in, about every 2.5 months, oldest first. Premium launches stay premium. The monthly pop-up re-releases stay intact. The newest 5 sets stay untouched. This is a parked asset being switched on, not a project to fund.

The single question for the product team We have a benefit players want, set levels for march size, that almost nobody buys as a lump sum but many would pay weekly. What is keeping us from selling it in installments through the store?
The cannibalization question, addressed

The likely concern is that selling levels in installments, and graduating older sets, eats premium launch-pack sales. It does not. The premium windows on the 10 older sets closed years ago and earn $0 today, so there is nothing there to cannibalize. The newest 5 sets stay premium and untouched. The installment buyers are the spenders who would never pay a lump sum of hundreds of dollars in the first place, so the weekly stream is net-new, not a discount on an existing sale. A reachable goal is precisely what keeps players topping up at all. On this view the change grows revenue rather than competing with it.
