Top War · Decor Set Store · A growth proposal

Nobody pays $875 for +6 march size. Plenty would pay $5 a week.

Completing a decoration set permanently raises a player's march size, and each duplicate full set levels that bonus higher. Today the only way to level a set is an expensive, time-limited launch pack, or, once the window closes, no way at all. That is a level-up almost nobody buys as a lump sum. The same upgrade, sold through the Decor Set Store in small weekly token installments toward a visible goal, is something a spender pays for gladly, week after week. This is a look at the recurring revenue and retention that the current design leaves on the table.

01
The upgrade players would pay for

The store cannot sell the one thing spenders want

Set leveling is a confirmed, permanent power mechanic. Complete every piece of a decoration set and that counts as Level 1: a flat +2 march size, applied for good, with no placement needed. Collect a second and third full copy of the same set and it climbs: Level 2 is +4, Level 3 is +6. Leveling up means collecting duplicate full sets, and that is precisely the kind of long, repeatable goal the token economy is built to feed.

Only two of the 23 sets can currently reach Level 3: Metal Music Fans (released May 2024) and Sweet Haven (released December 2025). The other 21 sets cap at Level 2, worth +4 march size. So the ceiling is modest and bounded: this is not an open-ended power spiral, it is a small permanent bonus that a dedicated player chases over many months.

Here is the problem. Leveling needs duplicate full sets, but the only place to buy full sets repeatedly and affordably, the token store, does not stock most of them. The expensive launch packs are the only paid path to level, and they vanish when a set's window closes. So a spender who would happily grind toward Level 2 of a set they love simply cannot, and the studio collects nothing.

02
The recurring revenue

A weekly habit beats a one-time wall

The value of an installment is not just that more people say yes. It is that the same person keeps saying yes. A spender who is leveling a set, or simply keeping a level to chase on the next, taps the $0.99 pack five times a week. That is about $4.95 a week, roughly $257 a year, and it renews every year for as long as there is something to grind toward.

Why the lifetime value is the point

A launch pack is a single transaction with a hard ceiling. A level-up sold in installments is an annuity: small, repeated, and renewing. The studio is not choosing between the two. The launch packs stay exactly as they are, and this installment revenue is additive on top, reaching the patient spender who was never going to pay full launch price anyway.

Across an illustrative 600 active players at about 45% paying, that is roughly $69,000 a year in installment revenue alone, scoped to the decoration token economy. Today that figure is close to zero, not because players would not pay, but because the store gives them nothing to pay toward.

Population, paying share and per-player spend are illustrative assumptions, exposed so they can be challenged. Additive to launch-pack revenue, never conflated with it.

One $875 wall earns once. A $5-a-week goal earns every week, for years.

03
The prerequisite

You can only grind a set you can reach

Installment leveling depends on one thing: the set has to be reachable in the token store so a player can collect it again and again. That is where today's catalogue gets in the way. The Decor Set Store sells sets for Decor Set Tokens, the soft currency players earn weekly and top up with small purchases. Of the 23 sets that exist, only these are currently buyable there:

In the affordable token store 8 SETS · ON ROTATION
Older sets shelved from the store 10 SETS · 2021 TO 2024 · THE BACKLOG
Recent sets: premium for now 5 SETS · PREMIUM PACKS · NOT CONTESTED

Access is the prerequisite that makes leveling possible. A player can only grind a set toward its max level once that set is reachable in the store. The freshest sets staying premium at launch is reasonable, and this proposal leaves that alone. The issue is the ten older sets, some dating to 2021 to 2023, that never reached the token store. Their paid window closed long ago, so today they cannot be leveled at all, by anyone, at any price.

The store only ever cycles the same eight on rotation. In fact, not one of the nine sets released since December 2023 has reached the token store, so the older backlog keeps growing while the same eight reshuffle. The supporting fix is simple: each time a new set launches, let the oldest shelved set graduate into the store, at the pace the studio already ships. That graduation is what unlocks the installment leveling above.

The store is built for a weekly drip

07
The behaviour

Two players, one trigger

Installment leveling works because both kinds of paying players respond to the same thing: a reward that's almost in reach.

The patient spender

Closes the gap a dollar at a time

Earns free tokens, tops up with the $1 pack five times a week to inch toward the next set or the next level. Low monthly spend, but reliable, month after month, as long as there's a goal to chase.

Fires when: a level-up is weeks away, not years, and never priced as one big wall.
The impatient spender

Pays to skip the wait

Sees a set or a level they don't have and buys the bigger pack to have it now. Higher spend per event, but only when the goal is actually reachable.

Fires when: there's something they don't have yet, within sight.

Price a level-up as one $875 wall and neither player says yes. Sell it by the week and both do.

08
The case

Sell the level-up players would actually buy

Letting players level sets through the store in small weekly installments isn't a giveaway. It's the version of this system where the studio collects recurring revenue it cannot reach today, and players get a fair, paced path to a small permanent bonus.

For the studio
  • Recurring revenue, not a one-time wall. A level-up sold at about $5 a week is an annuity of roughly $257 a year per spender, additive on top of launch packs.
  • It collects what the wall never could. Almost nobody pays $350 to $875 in a lump sum, so today that revenue is close to zero. Installments capture the players who would gladly pay over time.
  • Re-engages your best players. The ones who've collected everything are exactly the ones with a level still to chase, if you let them.
For players
  • A reachable upgrade. Each set level is a permanent +2 march size; an affordable, paced path lets newer and mid-tier players close the power gap instead of staring at a wall.
  • A reason to keep playing. A level filling on the horizon instead of a shelf they can only look at.
  • A fair path. Earned tokens and small spend both lead somewhere, at any budget.
The fair question: won't this undercut pack sales?

Short answer: no. The recent sets stay premium and keep their full launch packs, so launch revenue is untouched. The installment path still takes months of earning per level, so impatient players keep buying packs to skip the wait. The monthly pop-up packs bundle far more than a bare set, so they hold their pull too. Only the oldest sets become reachable, and only those reach a level a player can grind toward. This revenue is purely additive: it captures the patient spender who was never going to pay $350 to $875 up front in the first place, and turns that lost sale into a weekly habit.

The proposal
Let players reach a set's max level through the Decor Set Store, paid in small weekly token installments, instead of only via expensive time-limited launch packs or not at all.

To make it possible, let the oldest shelved set graduate into the store each time a new set launches (one in, one graduates), keeping the newest premium and the pop-up packs untouched. A +2 march level-up is a hard sell at a lump sum of hundreds of dollars, but an easy, repeated yes at about $5 a week toward a visible goal. That is sticky recurring revenue and stronger retention the current design simply throws away, additive to everything the studio already sells.
